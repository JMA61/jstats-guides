jstats Statistics in R, Made Accessible

About the developer: Jeff Ackerman

I'm a social scientist who teaches quantitative methods at Griffith University. I grew up in Pennsylvania, in the United States, and moved to Australia in 2013 to join Griffith; I'm now an Australian citizen. I studied statistics and research methods under several well-known methodologists, including C. R. Rao — himself a student of Ronald Fisher, the statistician who developed Analysis of Variance (ANOVA).

I built jstats to make statistical analysis in R approachable for social-science researchers and students. R is powerful, but its learning curve and rough edges can make the move from commercial statistical software like SPSS, Stata, or SAS intimidating. jstats smooths that path: shorter, more consistent syntax; sensible defaults; protection from some of base R's more confusing behaviours; and output styled after the conventions the expensive commercial platforms use, so results look familiar from the first run.

It stays close enough to base R that the skills you build transfer to the wider R world rather than locking you into a private dialect. jstats grew out of teaching statistics to social-science students who wanted to learn the methods, but not complex computer programming. Alongside statistics, I trained in computer programming at the university level decades ago, and that combination — now paired with modern AI-assisted software engineering and careful testing — is what makes it practical for one person to build and maintain a package this size. It's continuously being shaped by what students and colleagues run into when they put it to work.

If you’re not yet sure what an R “package” is, the Quick Start will give you a feel for it. In short, a package is an add-on toolset you install into R, and jstats is currently more than 23,000 lines of computer code – including its extensive help files – working behind the scenes, so that the commands you’re required to type stay short and intuitive, and the output looks more like what you’d see in SPSS or Stata. Using jstats in your own teaching?

You’re welcome to. jstats is under continuous development – I’m regularly adding and refining functions. My current focus is the set of analyses I cover in my course, but I plan to expand it with additional functions for other data-analysis tasks common in social-science research and publishing. For now it installs from GitHub, where the project lives during early development; before long I expect to move it to CRAN – R’s central package archive – so installation becomes simpler and more routine. If you’re using jstats in a course – at Griffith or anywhere else – I’d be glad to hear about it, purely out of interest and so I can get a sense of where the package is being used. Email me at SurveyCentre@griffith.edu.au.
